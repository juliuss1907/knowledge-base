# What Comes After Systems Thinking

## Summary

Systems thinking was a breakthrough in management frameworks, introducing feedback loops, nonlinearity, and emergent behavior. However, it has a boundary condition: it works well for complicated systems but struggles with complex systems where agents adapt and learn. This article presents three frameworks that extend systems thinking for complex terrain: Cynefin (a decision framework), Complex Adaptive Systems theory (explaining why complex systems behave differently), and Ashby's Law of Requisite Variety (structural principle for governance design).

## Key points

- Systems thinking excels in complicated environments but hits limits with complex adaptive systems
- Complicated systems have many parts with fixed rules; complex systems have adaptive agents that change behavior
- Cynefin framework distinguishes five domains, most importantly Complicated vs Complex
- In the Complex domain, cause and effect are only visible in retrospect, not predictable in advance
- Complex Adaptive Systems theory introduces emergence, nonlinearity, and co-evolution
- Emergence means system behavior arises from interactions, not predictable from individual agents
- Nonlinearity means small inputs can produce large outputs and vice versa
- Co-evolution means managers are inside the system, not outside observers
- Ashby's Law states: only variety can absorb variety — governance must match system complexity
- Adding more governance to complex systems often increases friction without solving the mismatch
- The progression is: systems thinking → Cynefin (diagnostic) → CAS theory (understanding) → Ashby's Law (design)

## Original excerpts

> "Systems thinking assumes, at some level, that the system can be understood. That if you map it carefully enough—the actors, the relationships, the feedback dynamics—you will have a model that gives you useful leverage."

> "A complex system is different in a precise way: the agents inside it adapt. They learn. They respond to your interventions by changing their behavior. The rules of the system evolve as the system runs."

> "The traffic jam is not in any individual car. The organizational culture is not in any individual employee. The project dynamic is not in any individual stakeholder."

> "Ashby's Law states: only variety can absorb variety. In plain terms: a controller can only manage a system if the controller has at least as many available responses as the system has possible states."

> "Together, they form a progression from mapping to navigating. From understanding systems to operating inside them. From managing plans to managing adaptive responses."

# Ashby's Law (Law of Requisite Variety)

## Definition

Ashby's Law of Requisite Variety, formulated by W. Ross Ashby in 1956 as part of foundational cybernetics work, states: only variety can absorb variety. It provides a structural principle for designing governance systems that can actually keep pace with the complexity of what they govern.

## Key ideas

- The law states: "only variety can absorb variety"
- A controller can only manage a system if it has at least as many available responses as the system has possible states
- If the governed system is more complex than the governance structure, regulation fails—not from bad intentions, but from structural mismatch
- Standard organizational response (more governance layers, reporting requirements, approval gates) often increases friction without solving the mismatch
- Requisite variety requires distributed decision-making authority and genuine team autonomy to respond to local conditions
- Well-governed complex projects may look "out of control" from outside because they're exercising the variety the situation requires
- Governance must be as varied as the problem system, not as streamlined and standardized as possible
- This is a direct challenge to most bureaucratic and federal project governance structures

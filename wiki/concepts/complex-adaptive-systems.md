# Complex Adaptive Systems (CAS)

## Definition

Complex Adaptive Systems theory, developed primarily at the Santa Fe Institute beginning in the 1980s, studies systems composed of agents that interact, learn, and adapt. It explains why complex systems behave differently than complicated ones and provides conceptual vocabulary for understanding emergent properties, nonlinearity, and co-evolution.

## Key ideas

- CAS studies systems where agents interact, learn, and adapt—not just follow fixed rules
- **Emergence**: System-level behavior arises from agent interactions in ways unpredictable from individual agents alone
- **Nonlinearity**: Small inputs can produce large outputs; large inputs can produce small ones—the relationship is not proportional
- **Co-evolution**: Agents don't just respond to the environment; they change it, which changes other agents, creating feedback loops
- In CAS, prediction is structurally limited—not from lack of information, but because behavior is genuinely underdetermined until it unfolds
- The manager is inside the system, not an outside observer—interventions become part of the system's inputs
- Examples: traffic jams (not in any individual car), organizational culture (not in any individual employee)
